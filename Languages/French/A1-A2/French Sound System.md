
An overview of how French sounds are organized — what's familiar, what's new, and where English and Portuguese speakers tend to struggle.

> [!info] Why this matters
> French pronunciation isn't "just reading with an accent." It has its own phonological logic — sounds that don't exist in English or Portuguese, rules about when letters go silent, and a rhythm that feels almost musical. Learning the system (not just individual words) is the fastest path to sounding natural.

---

## The Big Picture

French has roughly **36 phonemes** (distinct sounds), compared to ~44 in English and ~37 in Portuguese. The difference isn't the count — it's *which* sounds they are.

| Category | What's familiar | What's new |
|---|---|---|
| **Consonants** | Most are close to English | /ʁ/ (the French R), /ɲ/ (like *gn* in *lasagne*) |
| **Vowels** | /a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, /u/ have rough equivalents | Rounded front vowels: /y/, /ø/, /œ/ |
| **Nasal vowels** | Portuguese has these! | Not in English at all |
| **Liaison & elision** | — | Words blend together at boundaries |

---

## The Four Problem Areas (for English/Portuguese speakers)

### 1. The French R — /ʁ/
==Not a tongue R.== It's produced at the back of the throat, like a soft gargle.

> [!tip] Portuguese comparison
> If you speak European Portuguese, you already have a similar sound — the *r* in **carro** or at the start of words like **rio**. Brazilian Portuguese is closer but uses a different variant. Either way, this is the easiest "new" French sound for Portuguese speakers.

> [!warning] English speakers
> Don't use the English retroflex R (tongue curling back). It will sound wrong. Practice the French R in isolation: **rouge**, **rue**, **Paris**.

---

### 2. Rounded Front Vowels — /y/, /ø/, /œ/
These sounds don't exist in English at all. See [[French Vowels]] for a full breakdown.

Quick landmark:
- ==**u**== in *tu* = say "ee" but round your lips → /y/
- ==**eu**== in *feu* = say "ay" but round your lips → /ø/
- ==**eu**== in *peur* = say "eh" but round your lips → /œ/

---

### 3. Nasal Vowels
French has **4 nasal vowels** — vowels where air flows through the nose.

> [!tip] Portuguese advantage
> Portuguese has nasal vowels too (ã, em, im…). If you speak Portuguese, your ear and mouth already know how to do this. The French versions are slightly different, but you're starting far ahead of English speakers.

| French spelling | Sound | Example | Portuguese parallel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **an / en** | /ɑ̃/ | *enfant* | like *ã* in **maçã** |
| **in / im / ain** | /ɛ̃/ | *vin* | like *im* in **sim** |
| **on** | /ɔ̃/ | *bon* | no exact match |
| **un** | /œ̃/ | *un* (merging with /ɛ̃/ in modern French) | — |

> [!warning] Don't pronounce the N
> In a nasal vowel, the N is **not** a separate consonant — it just nasalizes the vowel before it. *Enfant* is not "en-FANT" — the whole first syllable is one nasal vowel sound.

---

### 4. Silent Letters & Liaison

French spelling and French pronunciation are two different things.

> [!info] The silent letter rule
> Final consonants are almost always **silent** in French — unless the next word starts with a vowel (that's **liaison**).
```
Normal:   vous [vu] — the S is silent
Liaison:  vous avez [vu-za-vé] — the S links to the next word's vowel
```

> [!example]
> - *petit* → [p'ti] — the T is silent
> - *petit ami* → [p'ti-ta-mi] — the T links (liaison)
> - *les enfants* → [lé-zɑ̃-fɑ̃] — the S of *les* links as a Z sound

> [!warning] Liaison traps
> Not all final consonants link — some liaisons are **forbidden**. This is a common intermediate-level mistake. See [[Liaison & Elision]] for the full rules.

## Rhythm & Stress

> [!info] French stress is almost flat
> English and Portuguese use **stress-timed rhythm** — some syllables are longer/louder. French is **syllable-timed**: every syllable gets roughly equal weight, with a slight rise at the end of a phrase.

> [!tip]
> Think of French as sounding more like a drumbeat (equal hits) than a heartbeat (LOUD-soft). This is why French sounds so smooth and fast to English ears — there are no "heavy" syllables to anchor onto.
