The vowel sounds of French — including the three rounded front vowels that don't exist in English.

> [!info] The core challenge
> French has more vowel distinctions than English, and three of them — /y/, /ø/, /œ/ — require a mouth position English speakers have never used. Portuguese speakers have a slight edge with nasal vowels but still need to learn the rounded front vowels.

## The Vowel Map

French vowels are organized by two axes:
- **Where** in the mouth (front → back)
- **Lip position** (spread / neutral / rounded)

| | Spread / Neutral | Rounded |
|---|---|---|
| **High-front** | /i/ — *si* | /y/ — *tu* |
| **Mid-front** | /e/ — *été*, /ɛ/ — *fête* | /ø/ — *feu*, /œ/ — *peur* |
| **Low** | /a/ — *patte* | — |
| **Back** | — | /o/ — *beau*, /ɔ/ — *or*, /u/ — *ou* |

## The Familiar Vowels

These have close enough equivalents that you can work with them from day one:

| French | Sound | Example | English parallel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **i** | /i/ | *lit* (bed) | "ee" in *see* |
| **é** | /e/ | *été* (summer) | "ay" in *say* (but shorter, no glide) |
| **è / ê** | /ɛ/ | *fête* (party) | "eh" in *bed* |
| **a** | /a/ | *patte* (paw) | "ah" in *spa* |
| **o** | /o/ or /ɔ/ | *beau* / *or* | "oh" / "aw" |
| **ou** | /u/ | *ou* (or) | "oo" in *food* |

> [!warning] The /e/ vs /ɛ/ distinction
> English mostly merges these. In French, ==**é**== (closed e) vs ==**è/ê**== (open e) can change meaning:
> - *chanter* (to sing) vs *chanté* (sung) — same spelling pattern, different vowels

## The Three New Vowels

These are the ones that take real practice. There are **no equivalents** in English.

### /y/ — written as **u**
==The single most common "new" sound for English speakers.==

**How to make it:**
1. Say the English "ee" (as in *see*)
2. Keep your tongue exactly there — high and front
3. Round your lips as if saying "oo"
```
Tongue position = /i/  +  Lip rounding = /u/  →  /y/
```

> [!example]
> - *tu* (you) — [ty]
> - *rue* (street) — [ʁy]
> - *lune* (moon) — [lyn]
> - *vu* (seen) — [vy]

> [!tip] Portuguese speakers
> The *u* in Brazilian Portuguese is already quite rounded. European Portuguese speakers: your unstressed *u* (as in *tudo*) is close. Neither is identical, but you're closer than English speakers.

> [!warning] Don't confuse with /u/
> French **u** = /y/ (rounded front). French **ou** = /u/ (rounded back, like English "oo").
> - *tu* [ty] ≠ *tout* [tu]
> - *vu* [vy] ≠ *vous* [vu]

---

### /ø/ — written as **eu** or **œu** (in open syllables)

**How to make it:**
1. Say the English "ay" (as in *say*) — or French /e/
2. Round your lips
```
Tongue position = /e/  +  Lip rounding  →  /ø/
```

> [!example]
> - *feu* (fire) — [fø]
> - *jeu* (game) — [ʒø]
> - *deux* (two) — [dø]
> - *bleu* (blue) — [blø]

---

### /œ/ — written as **eu** or **œ** (in closed syllables)

The open version of /ø/. The distinction is mostly determined by the syllable structure.

**How to make it:**
1. Say "eh" (as in *bed*) — or French /ɛ/
2. Round your lips
```
Tongue position = /ɛ/  +  Lip rounding  →  /œ/
```

> [!example]
> - *peur* (fear) — [pœʁ]
> - *cœur* (heart) — [kœʁ]
> - *heure* (hour) — [œʁ]
> - *seul* (alone) — [sœl]

> [!info] When is it /ø/ vs /œ/?
> **Rough rule:** if the syllable ends in a vowel sound (open syllable) → /ø/. If it ends in a consonant sound (closed syllable) → /œ/.
> - *feu* → open → /fø/
> - *peur* → closed (ends in /ʁ/) → /pœʁ/

## Vowel Pairs & Digraphs

Many French vowel sounds are written as two letters:

| Spelling | Sound | Example | Watch out |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ou** | /u/ | *vous* | Not like English "ou" in *out* |
| **au / eau** | /o/ | *beau, gauche* | Always /o/, never /ɔ/ |
| **eu / œu** | /ø/ or /œ/ | *feu, cœur* | Depends on syllable type |
| **ai / ei** | /ɛ/ | *mais, neige* | Like è, not like English "ai" |
| **oi** | /wa/ | *moi, voix* | Two sounds fused: /w/ + /a/ |

> [!example] **oi** in action
> - *moi* (me) — [mwa]
> - *toi* (you) — [twa]
> - *voilà* — [vwa-la]

> [!tip] Portuguese speakers
> The *oi* sound in French (/wa/) is different from Portuguese *oi* (/oj/). Don't carry the Portuguese pronunciation over.
