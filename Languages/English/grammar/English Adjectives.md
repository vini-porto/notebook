## Core Concept

Adjectives modify nouns or describe a subject through a linking verb. 

## Key Rules & Patterns

### Position: Attributive vs. Predicative

- **Attributive** adjectives come _directly before_ the noun they modify: 
>_a tall man_, _an old wooden house

- **Predicative** adjectives come _after a linking verb_ (be, seem, become, look, feel, remain, appear): 
>He is tall_, _The room looks clean_

- Most adjectives can occupy both positions, but some are **position-restricted**:

    - **Predicative-only** (cannot precede a noun): _afraid, asleep, alive, awake, aware, alone, glad, ill_ (when meaning "unwell"), _poorly_, _sorry_ (in the apology sense)
        - ✗ _an afraid boy_ → ✓ _a frightened boy_
        - ✗ _an asleep child_ → ✓ _a sleeping child

    - **Attributive-only** (rarely follow a linking verb with the same meaning): _former, main, utter, sheer, mere, atomic, earthen_
        - ✗ _The reason was main_ → ✓ _the main reason_

> [!tip] Advanced note 
> Some adjectives _change meaning_ depending on position. 
> - _A poor woman_ (attributive) = an unfortunate woman. 
> - _The woman is poor_ (predicative) = she has little money. 
>
>>[!example] Similarly: 
>>- _a certain answer_ (a specific answer) vs. _she is certain_ (she is sure). 
>>- _The present members_ (those who are here now) vs. _the members are present_.

### Adjective Order (OSASCOMP)

When stacking multiple adjectives before a noun, the canonical sequence is:

**Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose (+ noun)**

Mnemonic: **OSASCOMP**

>_a beautiful little old round green French silver whittling knife_

- In practice, rarely more than three adjectives are stacked; native speakers violate the order occasionally for emphasis
- When two adjectives belong to the **same category**, use _and_: _a blue and green cotton shirt_

> [!tip] Advanced note 
> The deeper logic behind OSASCOMP is that adjectives closer to the noun are more _classifying_ (objective, intrinsic), while those further away are more _evaluative_ (subjective, speaker-dependent). 
> 
> Opinion comes first because it is the least inherent quality of the object. This is why reordering feels wrong it disrupts the gradient from subjectivity to objectivity.

### Gradable vs. Non-Gradable Adjectives

**Gradable adjectives** describe qualities that exist on a scale; they accept grading adverbs (_very, quite, extremely, fairly, rather, slightly, a bit_):

- _cold → a bit cold / very cold / extremely cold_

**Non-gradable adjectives** fall into three subtypes:

| Subtype     | Description               | Examples                                                  | Correct adverbs                                  |
| ----------- | ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Extreme     | Already imply "very"      | _freezing, furious, exhausted, enormous, tiny, terrified_ | _absolutely, completely, utterly, really_        |
| Absolute    | Binary states (either/or) | _dead, perfect, unique, married, pregnant, finished_      | _absolutely, completely, virtually, practically_ |
| Classifying | Assign to a category      | _nuclear, domestic, environmental, chemical, French_      | _almost, largely, primarily, exclusively_        |

- ✗ _very freezing_ / ✗ _very dead_ / ✗ _very nuclear_
- ✓ _absolutely freezing_ / ✓ _completely dead_ / ✓ _largely domestic_

>[!warning] Exception
> _really_ and _pretty_ collocate with both gradable and non-gradable adjectives, use them when unsure

- Non-gradable adjectives do **not** normally form comparatives or superlatives (✗ _more unique_, ✗ _deader_)

> [!warning] 
> _Unique_ is technically absolute (either one of a kind or not), but native speakers routinely use _very unique_ in informal speech. In formal writing or academic registers, avoid it.

### Predicative Adjective + Preposition Collocations

Many predicative adjectives require a specific preposition when followed by a complement. These are **not** optional choices — wrong prepositions sound unidiomatic:

- _afraid of / interested in / proud of / responsible for / aware of / capable of / keen on / similar to / different from_

## Collocations & Fixed Expressions

- **absolutely + extreme adjective** — the standard intensifier for non-gradable extremes (_absolutely exhausted, absolutely furious_)
- **completely + absolute adjective** — used with binary states (_completely wrong, completely silent_)
- **utterly + negative extreme adjective** — formal/emphatic register (_utterly ridiculous, utterly absurd_)
- **rather + gradable adjective** — slightly formal or ironic tone (_rather strange, rather impressive_)
- **fairly + gradable adjective** — moderate; less common in informal speech (_fairly good, fairly straightforward_)
- **quite** — ambiguous: before gradable = _moderately_ (_quite good_ = not very good); before absolute/non-gradable = _completely_ (_quite perfect, quite impossible_) — a frequent learner trap
- **pretty + adjective** — informal spoken; interchangeable with _fairly_ in most contexts (_pretty tired, pretty obvious_)
- **a bit + negative gradable adjective** — softening device, common in polite speech (_a bit boring, a bit expensive_)

> [!example] 
> "It was quite good" (gradable) ≠ "It was quite perfect" (non-gradable). 
> 
> The first means _reasonably good_; the second means _completely perfect_. Context and stress carry the distinction in speech.

## Written Examples

1. _She found a beautiful old Italian marble fountain in the courtyard._ (opinion → age → origin → material)
2. _The solution is not merely difficult — it's absolutely impossible._ (gradable vs. extreme non-gradable contrast)
3. _He seemed completely unaware of the implications of his decision._ (predicative + absolute adverb + prep. complement)
4. _The former minister is now responsible for the new oversight body._ (attributive-only _former_ + predicative + prep.)
5. ✗ _She was very exhausted after the flight._ → ✓ _She was absolutely exhausted after the flight._

## Register & Nuance

- **Extreme adjectives** (_furious, devastated, stunning, hilarious_) are more common in **informal spoken** English, especially with _absolutely_ or _really_. Overusing them in formal writing reads as hyperbole
- **Quite** is a register minefield: British and American speakers use it differently. In British English, _quite good_ often faintly _damns with praise_ (= acceptable, not impressive). In American English, _quite good_ often means _very good_. When writing for mixed audiences, prefer _fairly_ or _rather_ for the moderate meaning
- **Utterly** has a markedly formal or literary weight. In academic or journalistic prose, it adds gravity (_utterly unsupported, utterly transformed_)
- **Predicative-only adjectives** (_afraid, asleep, alive_) have Old English roots (the _a-_ prefix is from Old English _an-_). Using their attributive equivalents (_frightened, sleeping, living_) is not just grammatically safe — it often sounds more formal
- Stacking more than three adjectives before a noun is felt as either literary/humorous or clumsy, depending on context

## Pronunciation & Prosody

- In multi-adjective strings, **sentence stress falls on the adjective closest to the noun** (the most classifying one): _a beautiful OLD French TABLE_ — stress on _old_, _French_, and especially _table_
- **Gradable adjectives** in predicative position receive strong stress when contrasted: _It wasn't COLD — it was FREEZING_
- **Reduced forms**: _absolute_ before a noun is often reduced in fast speech to /ˈæbsələt/, and _absolutely_ to /ˈæbsəluːtli/ or even /ˈæbsəli/
- When _quite_ carries the "completely" meaning (with non-gradable adjectives), it is often **stressed**: _It was QUITE impossible_ (British English, formal)
- When _quite_ carries the weaker "moderately" meaning, it is typically **unstressed**: _It was quite /kwɪt/ good_
- The _-ed_ / _-ing_ adjective distinction (_bored/boring_, _tired/tiring_) is a prosodic trap: _-ed_ forms carry the suffix stress slightly and end on a consonant cluster; mispronouncing _bored_ as /ˈbɔːrɪd/ (adding a vowel) is a common intermediate-level error

## Native-like Upgrade

- Learner version: _The movie was very funny._ Native version: _The movie was absolutely hilarious._ / _It was hysterical._ (extreme adjective replaces intensifier + gradable)
    
- Learner version: _She was very tired after working all day._ Native version: _She was completely drained after a full day's work._ / _She was utterly shattered._ (British informal)
    
- Learner version: _It was very cold outside._ Native version: _It was absolutely freezing out there._ (colloquial) / _It was bitterly cold._ (strong collocation, more formal)
    
- Learner version: _He is a big, nice man._ Native version: _He's a big, friendly guy._ / _He's a big warm guy._ (adjective choice + order refinement)
    
- Learner version: _I am very aware about the risks._ Native version: _I'm well aware of the risks._ (_well_ is the standard intensifier for _aware_, not _very_; also note prep. _of_, not _about_)

## Vocabulary Index

- [[attributive adjective]] — grammar term, adjective placed directly before the noun it modifies
- [[predicative adjective]] — grammar term, adjective following a linking verb, describing the subject
- [[postpositive adjective]] — adjective placed immediately _after_ a noun; found in legal phrases, fixed expressions, or borrowed from Romance languages (_attorney general, body politic_)
- [[gradable adjective]] — adjective describing a quality on a scale; accepts grading adverbs (_very, quite, extremely_)
- [[non-gradable adjective]] — adjective describing an absolute, extreme, or classifying quality; does not accept grading adverbs like _very_
- [[extreme adjective]] — subtype of non-gradable; already implies _very_ in its meaning (_freezing, furious, exhausted, enormous, hilarious_)
- [[absolute adjective]] — subtype of non-gradable; describes a binary state (_dead, perfect, unique, finished_)
- [[classifying adjective]] — subtype of non-gradable; assigns to a category (_nuclear, domestic, chemical, French_)
- [[grading adverb]] — adverb that modifies gradable adjectives by varying degree (_very, extremely, fairly, quite, rather, slightly, a bit_)
- [[intensifier]] — broad term for an adverb that strengthens an adjective (_absolutely, completely, utterly, totally_)
- [[OSASCOMP]] — mnemonic for the canonical order of multiple adjectives: Opinion–Size–Age–Shape–Colour–Origin–Material–Purpose
- [[linking verb]] — verb connecting subject to a predicative adjective (also: copular verb); includes _be, seem, become, look, feel, appear, remain, sound, taste, smell_
- [[quite]] — adverb; means _moderately_ with gradable adjectives (British English) but _completely_ with non-gradable adjectives — context and stress disambiguate
- [[utterly]] — adverb, formal/emphatic intensifier for non-gradable adjectives, particularly negative ones
- [[rather]] — adverb; slightly formal or ironic when modifying gradable adjectives; often implies mild surprise or understatement
- [[well aware]] — fixed collocation; _well_ is the idiomatic intensifier for _aware_ (not _very_)
- [[bitterly cold]] — strong fixed collocation; the adverb _bitterly_ collocates almost exclusively with cold-related adjectives and feelings of resentment

## Memory Hook

**Grading adverb + non-gradable adjective = social signal**: saying _very freezing_ or _very exhausted_ doesn't just break a rule — it signals an intermediate speaker to a native ear. The fix is simple: swap _very_ for _absolutely_ when the adjective is already extreme, and the sentence immediately sounds native.
