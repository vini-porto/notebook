# Core Concept

Adjectives modify nouns and pronouns by attributing qualities, quantities, or categories to them. 

# Key Rules & Patterns

- Adjectives in English are **invariable**, they do not inflect for gender, number, or case. This is unlike Portuguese (_bonito / bonita / bonitos / bonitas_), so no agreement errors should occur, but learners still occasionally hypercorrect.

- Adjectives appear in two positions:
    - **Attributive** — directly before the noun: _a tired candidate_
    - **Predicative** — after a linking verb: _The candidate looked tired
    
- The most common linking verbs that take predicative adjectives: **be, look, seem, feel, sound, taste, smell, appear, become, get, remain, stay, turn**
- The question test: adjectives typically answer _What kind of...?_, _Which one?_, _How many?_, or _Whose?_

- Adjective categories from the source: **numeric, quantitative, qualitative, possessive, interrogative, demonstrative**

- Articles (_a, an, the_) and possessives (_my, your, their_) function grammatically as adjectives — important for syntactic analysis

- Nouns can be repurposed as adjectives in **noun adjuncts**: _a glass vase_, _a metal tray_, _a sleeping bag_

> [!tip] Advanced note 
> The attributive vs. predicative distinction is not merely positional some adjectives are **position-restricted**. 
> _[[Utter]]_, _[[mere]]_, _[[sheer]]_ are attributive-only (_utter nonsense_ ✓ / _the nonsense was utter_ ✗). _Afraid_, _asleep_, _alive_, _awake_ are predicative-only (_the asleep child_ ✗ / _the child was asleep_ ✓). This is a common C1/C2 test point.

> [!tip] Advanced note Noun adjuncts (_a sleeping bag_, _a morning run_) differ from participial adjectives (_a sleeping child_). In _sleeping bag_, _sleeping_ describes **purpose**, not an ongoing action. Native speakers process this automatically; learners sometimes over-apply the participial reading.

## 3. Collocations & Fixed Expressions

**fact adjective** — objective, verifiable quality; not open to debate (e.g., _circular_, _wooden_, _German_) **opinion adjective** — subjective evaluation; varies by speaker (e.g., _horrid_, _beautiful_, _silly_) **long and blonde** — adjectives joined by _and_ when listing two qualities of equal weight **long, blonde hair** — comma-separated stacking (see [[Adjective Order]] for the full rule) **used to** — treated as a quasi-adjective construction; has dedicated grammar rules (see [[Used To]]) **a sleeping bag** — noun adjunct: adjective = purpose, NOT the act of sleeping **looks tired / seems tired / feels tired** — predicative patterns after linking verbs; natural in spoken and written English

> [!tip] Advanced note In formal written English, stacked pre-nominal adjectives are frequent: _a long, narrow, dimly lit corridor_. In casual speech, native speakers tend to split these across clauses: _the corridor was long and narrow, and really badly lit_. Knowing when to stack vs. split is a register skill.

## 5. Register & Nuance

- **Attributive adjectives** are more common in **formal writing** — they create a denser, more economical noun phrase: _the unprecedented economic collapse_ (vs. _the economic collapse, which was unprecedented_)
- **Predicative adjectives** are more natural in **spoken English** and informal writing — they follow the subject-verb pattern of natural speech
- **Opinion adjectives** carry social weight: calling something _horrid_ or _ridiculous_ encodes the speaker's subjectivity, which can be a face-threatening act in professional contexts — hedge them (_slightly_, _arguably_, _rather_) when needed
- Adjective-heavy prose can feel **over-written** in English. Unlike Portuguese, where a richly modified noun phrase often signals sophistication, in English — especially in journalism and academic writing — **precision beats density**. One well-chosen adjective outweighs three generic ones

> [!warning] False friend alert: Portuguese _polido_ (polished/polite) ≠ _polished_ in English, which can mean literally smooth or figuratively well-rehearsed. Context-dependent; don't assume semantic equivalence with seemingly obvious cognates.

## 6. Pronunciation & Prosody

- In **noun phrases**, the adjective typically carries **primary stress** when it's the key information: _a WOODEN cupboard_ (material matters) vs. _a wooden CUPBOARD_ (type of furniture matters)
- In **predicative position**, the adjective nearly always receives focus stress: _She looked EXHAUSTED_
- Linking verb + adjective reductions in natural speech:
    - _It was a hot day_ → /ɪtwəzə **hɒt** deɪ/ — _was_ reduced to /wəz/
    - _She looks tired_ → the /s/ in _looks_ links directly to /t/ in _tired_: /ʃə **lʊkst**aɪəd/
- Intonation: in lists of adjectives, rising intonation on each item signals continuation; falling intonation on the final adjective marks the end of the description — _tall↗, dark↗, and handsome↘_

## 7. Native-like Upgrade

- **Learner version:** _It was a very big and impressive building._
    
- **Native version:** _It was an imposing building._ — One precise adjective replaces a vague stacked pair
    
- **Learner version:** _The weather was hot and the day was long._
    
- **Native version:** _It was a long, sweltering day._ — Compression into a single noun phrase is more idiomatic in descriptive prose
    
- **Learner version:** _She is a person who is very intelligent and serious._
    
- **Native version:** _She's sharp and no-nonsense._ — Informal register; collocations replace generic adjectives
    
- **Learner version:** _The box is square-shaped._
    
- **Native version:** _The box is square._ — Shape adjectives already encode the concept; _-shaped_ is redundant unless the shape is approximate or unusual (_roughly diamond-shaped_)
    

> [!example] The real native-speaker skill isn't knowing more adjectives — it's knowing **which one to cut**. English style guides consistently warn against adjective stacking. If you can remove an adjective without losing meaning, do it.

## 8. Practice

**Exercise 1 — Register Transformation** Rewrite this informal description in formal written English, adjusting adjective placement and register:

> _"The guy was really tall and kind of intimidating, and the room felt cold and weird."_

**Exercise 2 — Error Correction** Find and fix the error(s) in the following sentence:

> _"It was an absolute and utter waste of everyone's precious and valuable time."_

**Exercise 3 — Compression Challenge** Reduce each of the following to a single well-chosen adjective or a tightly constructed noun phrase. Avoid repeating the original words.

> a) _"A speech that nobody could argue with"_ b) _"A decision that had never been seen before"_ c) _"A building that is falling apart and not safe to enter"_

### Answer Key

**Exercise 1 — Suggested answer:** _"The individual was notably tall and carried an authoritative presence; the room felt cold and unsettling."_ Note: predicative adjectives are fine in formal writing — the key shift is from vague intensifiers (_really_, _kind of_) to precise modifiers.

**Exercise 2:** _"Absolute"_ and _"utter"_ are near-synonyms here — using both is redundant. Similarly, _"precious"_ and _"valuable"_ overlap. Native phrasing: _"It was an utter waste of everyone's time."_ or _"It was an absolute waste of valuable time."_

**Exercise 3 — Suggested answers:** a) _an irrefutable / unassailable speech_ b) _an unprecedented decision_ c) _a derelict building_

## 9. Real-World Examples

- Nature and travel documentaries (e.g., _Planet Earth_ narration) are excellent models for **attributive adjective stacking** done well — the writing is precise, economical, and the adjectives are always purposeful rather than decorative.
- Opinion journalism in _The Guardian_ or _The Atlantic_ frequently uses **predicative adjective constructions** after verbs like _seem_, _remain_, _prove_ — worth studying how these writers hedge opinion adjectives with adverbials.

---

## 10. Memory Hook

> **Attributive = embedded before the noun (built in); Predicative = separated after the verb (standing apart).** If the adjective _sits inside_ the noun phrase, it's attributive. If it _comes after_ a linking verb and describes the subject, it's predicative. Two jobs, two positions — never confused again.
