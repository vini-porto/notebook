## Core Concept

[[Adjectives]] are far more than simple [[describing words]]: at C1 level, mastery means controlling [[adjective order]], distinguishing [[gradable]] from [[ungradable]] adjectives, choosing the correct [[intensifier]], and knowing which [[collocation|collocations]] are fixed and which are flexible. The gap between _grammatically correct_ and _natively natural_ is widest here.

---

## Key Rules & Patterns

### [[Adjective order]] — OSASCOMP

When multiple [[attributive adjective|attributive adjectives]] (pre-nominal) stack before a noun, the canonical sequence is:

- **Opinion** → **Size** → **Age** → **Shape** → **Colour** → **Origin** → **Material** → **Purpose**
- Mnemonic: **OSASCOMP** _(Opinion, Size, Age, Shape, Colour, Origin, Material, Purpose)_
- [[Determiner|Determiners]] (a, the, some, her…) always precede the entire adjective stack
- [[Intensifier|Intensifiers]] and [[adverb|adverbs]] (really, very, quite) sit after the determiner, before the first adjective

> [!tip] Advanced note OSASCOMP is a tendency, not an inviolable law. What drives order at a deeper level is a **subjectivity gradient**: the more a quality is _perceived_ (opinion), the further from the noun it sits; the more it is _inherent_ to the object (material, purpose), the closer it hugs the noun. This is why collocating adjectives — pairs that conventionally travel together — often override the mechanical OSASCOMP sequence. Native speakers do not recite the mnemonic; they feel the gradient.

- Using **more than three** [[pre-modifier|pre-modifiers]] before a single noun is rare outside literary or rhetorical prose
- [[Predicate adjective|Predicate adjectives]] (post-verbal, following [[linking verb|linking verbs]] like _be, seem, become, remain_) are not subject to ordering rules
- When two or more adjectives belong to the **same category**, connect them with **and**: _a blue and green flag_; when they are from different categories, use a comma or no separator

### [[Gradable]] vs [[Ungradable]] adjectives

**Gradable adjectives** describe qualities on a sliding scale and accept degree [[modifier|modifiers]]:

- Modifiers: _very, extremely, quite, rather, slightly, a bit, fairly, pretty_
- Example: _quite angry → very angry → extremely angry_

**Ungradable adjectives** fall into two subtypes:

|Subtype|Description|Correct modifier|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Extreme**|Already encodes intensity (_= very + gradable base_)|_absolutely, utterly, completely, really_|_absolutely furious_ (≠ ~~very furious~~)|
|**Absolute**|Binary state — either true or not|_completely, entirely, virtually_|_completely dead, virtually impossible_|
|**Classifying**|Indicates type membership, not degree|_completely, entirely_|_entirely digital, completely foreign (policy)_|

> [!warning] The modifiers _very_ and _extremely_ **do not collocate** with extreme or absolute adjectives: ~~very furious~~, ~~extremely exhausted~~ are marked as learner errors. The safe universal modifier is **really**, which works with both gradable and ungradable adjectives in informal speech.

> [!tip] Advanced note Some adjectives are context-sensitive: **full** is ungradable (_the hotel is full_) but can become gradable in colloquial comparisons (_the hotel is quite full tonight — you might still get a room_). Other ambiguous cases include _empty, beautiful, new, possible, black_. The deciding factor is whether the speaker is treating the quality as an **absolute threshold** or a **relative position on a scale**.

---

### [[Post-positive adjective|Post-positive adjectives]]

A small class of adjectives only appears **after** the noun they modify — this is not an error but a [[lexicalised]] pattern:

- _the body politic, time immemorial, heir apparent, attorney general, president elect_
- Common in formal, legal, and institutional [[register]]
- In everyday speech, this pattern survives mainly in fixed [[noun phrase|noun phrases]]

## Collocations & Fixed Expressions

> [!example] Adjective–noun collocations that trip up advanced learners

- **strong** argument / coffee / wind — not ~~powerful argument~~, ~~intense coffee~~
- **heavy** rain / traffic / smoker — not ~~strong rain~~, ~~big traffic~~
- **deep** concern / sleep / trouble — not ~~big concern~~, ~~strong trouble~~
- **high** expectations / standards / risk — not ~~big expectations~~
- **bright** idea / future — not ~~brilliant idea~~ (register shift: _brilliant_ is evaluative, not collocational)
- **great** happiness / success / deal — not ~~big happiness~~ (Portuguese _grande_ maps loosely to _great_, but the English [[collocation]] is not one-to-one)
- **utter** nonsense / devastation / failure — near-synonym of _complete_ but considerably stronger in register; typically written/formal

**Adverb–adjective collocations (intensifier fixed pairings):**

- **deeply** concerned / troubled / moved — _deeply_ signals emotional or ethical weight; formal register
- **highly** unlikely / competitive / controversial — formal, written; common in journalism and academic prose
- **bitterly** cold / disappointed / ironic — [[emotive intensifier]], often negative connotation
- **blindingly** obvious — [[idiomatic intensifier]]; colloquial but vivid; always slightly sardonic in tone
- **perfectly** reasonable / clear / normal — downplays unexpectedness; used to defuse tension in spoken British English

## Written Examples

1. She gave a **brief, dismissive** wave and left. _(two opinion adjectives — note no OSASCOMP conflict when both are the same type)_
2. He bought an **extraordinary little old silver** pocket watch at the flea market. _(Opinion → Size → Age → Material)_
3. The results were **absolutely conclusive** — no further testing was needed. _(ungradable absolute + correct modifier)_
4. There is **deep concern** among regulators about the pace of AI deployment. _(fixed adverb–adjective collocation, formal register)_
5. ✗ The situation is **very impossible** to resolve without external mediation. → ✓ The situation is **virtually impossible** / **completely impossible** to resolve without external mediation. _(impossible = absolute adjective; very is incompatible)_

## Register & Nuance

**Spoken vs written:**

- In [[spoken English]], native speakers freely break the OSASCOMP sequence for rhythm or [[emphasis]]: _"that horrible big dog"_ is perfectly natural even if _big horrible dog_ is the canonical order
- [[Predicate adjective|Predicate position]] (_the film was absolutely brilliant_) is pragmatically neutral across spoken and written registers
- [[Attributive adjective|Attributive stacking]] of three or more adjectives reads as literary or rhetorical; in casual speech it sounds overwrought

**Intensifier register map:**

|Modifier|Register|Variety|
|---|---|---|
|_very_|Neutral|Both AmE and BrE|
|_quite_|Ambiguous (see below)|Primarily BrE|
|_rather_|Formal / literary|Primarily BrE|
|_pretty_|Informal|Both, but especially BrE|
|_fairly_|Neutral-formal|Both|
|_somewhat_|Formal / written|Primarily AmE|
|_utterly_|Strong emphasis; formal or ironic|Primarily BrE|

> [!warning] The **quite** trap In **British English**, _quite_ has two distinct uses depending on the adjective it modifies:
> 
> - With **gradable** adjectives: _quite_ = _fairly / moderately_ → _"It's quite good"_ = moderate praise, not strong
> - With **ungradable** adjectives: _quite_ = _completely / absolutely_ → _"It's quite perfect"_ = total agreement
> 
> **American English** uses _quite_ to mean _very_ regardless of adjective type. A BrE speaker saying _"quite interesting"_ to an AmE listener will be significantly misunderstood. This is a live interference point even for C1 learners.

## Pronunciation & Prosody

- In [[noun phrase|noun phrases]] with multiple adjectives, **primary stress falls on the noun**, with secondary stress on the closest [[opinion adjective]]: _a beau**ti**ful old house_ (stress: _beau-_, _house_)
- [[Predicate adjective|Predicate adjectives]] after _be_ carry the [[tonic syllable]] in [[unmarked]] sentences: _The film was ab-so-lute-ly **bril**-liant_
- The modifier _really_ is typically reduced in fast speech: /ˈrɪli/ → /rɪl/ — "It was really awful" sounds like "It was rilly awful"
- _Quite_ in BrE is often produced with a slight **fall-rise** intonation when used as a hedge (_"It's quite good"_ = I'm not fully committing); a flat fall signals the absolute reading (_"That's quite perfect"_)
- The intensifier _utterly_ is typically spoken with deliberate, slightly slowed articulation — the length is part of its performative force

## Native-like Upgrade

- **Learner version:** The speech was very long and very boring. **Native version:** The speech was _interminably long_ and _mind-numbingly boring_. _(compound-adverb intensifiers; far more expressive)_
    
- **Learner version:** I was very surprised by the result. **Native version:** I was _absolutely stunned_ by the result. _(upgrades gradable + very → extreme adjective + correct intensifier)_
    
- **Learner version:** It was a very important and very old document. **Native version:** It was a _hugely significant, centuries-old_ document. _(collapses two weak intensifier pairs into one tighter noun phrase)_
    
- **Learner version:** He is very smart but quite proud. **Native version:** He is _razor-sharp_ but _insufferably arrogant_. _([[compound adjective]] + precise [[connotation]])_
    
- **Learner version:** The coffee is very strong and it's hot. **Native version:** The coffee is _strong_ — and it's _scalding_. _(drop _very_ before the right collocation; use extreme adjective for temperature)_

## Real-World Examples

The [[gradable]] vs [[ungradable]] distinction is constantly audible in native English podcasts — hosts routinely use _absolutely_ before extreme adjectives (_absolutely exhausted_, _absolutely furious_) and _really_ as the catch-all intensifier, almost never _very_ in informal speech. Shows like _The Rest Is Politics_ (BrE political commentary) demonstrate the BrE _quite_ ambiguity in action, where hosts modulate between the hedging and the absolute uses fluidly.

The OSASCOMP order is best heard in narrative fiction audiobooks (e.g., any Booker Prize longlist title) where authors deliberately use three-adjective stacks for stylistic effect — the order feels invisible precisely because it is obeyed.

## Memory Hook

> **Opinion first, material last — subjectivity fades as you approach the noun.** And: if you'd say _very_ before it in normal speech, it's [[gradable]]. If _very_ sounds wrong, reach for _absolutely_.
